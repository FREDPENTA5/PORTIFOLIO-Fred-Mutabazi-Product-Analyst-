East African Job Market Analysis

Problem

A job platform needed to prioritise where to grow: which sectors and geographies had the strongest demand, where supply was falling short, and where product and sales efforts would have the highest impact. Without a clear view of the market, resource allocation was based on assumptions rather than data.

Methodology

I analysed job posting and application data across Kenya, Uganda, and Tanzania over 12 months. I grouped postings by sector (e.g. Software & Data, Sales & Marketing, Finance, Healthcare, Education) and by country, and compared posting volume to application volume to identify supply–demand gaps. I also looked at experience-level mix (entry-level vs mid vs senior) to spot underserved segments.

Data sources included internal platform events (postings, applications, views), plus publicly available labour and sector reports for context. Analysis was done in SQL and Python; visualisations in Tableau.

Key findings

  • Tech and Sales & Marketing had the highest posting volume; Healthcare and Education showed the highest applications per posting, indicating strong demand relative to supply.
  • Kenya accounted for roughly 45% of postings; Uganda and Tanzania showed faster quarter-on-quarter growth, suggesting room to grow there.
  • Entry-level roles had about 2.1× more applications per posting than senior roles, pointing to concentrated demand and potential value in better matching and employer acquisition in that segment.
  • Finance and Customer Service showed a clear supply–demand gap: high application volume but moderate posting growth, so prioritising employer acquisition in these sectors could pay off.

Recommendations

  • Prioritise employer acquisition and success in Healthcare, Education, and Finance to balance supply with demand.
  • Build segment-specific landing and search experiences for high-intent categories (e.g. entry-level, tech) to improve match quality and conversion.
  • Invest in Uganda and Tanzania with localised content and outreach, given their growth in postings and relative under-penetration.
