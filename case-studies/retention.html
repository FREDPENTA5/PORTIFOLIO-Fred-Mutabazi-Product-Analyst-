User Behavior

Job Seeker Retention Analysis

Problem

Job seeker 30-day retention was below target. The product and growth teams needed to know where users dropped off, which cohorts were at risk, and what actions (e.g. email, in-product nudges, UX changes) would have the biggest impact on retention.

Methodology

I built cohorts by signup week and tracked retention (return visit or application within 7, 14, and 30 days). I analysed session depth, pages per session, and application rate by cohort and by acquisition channel. I also mapped the journey from landing → search → view → apply to find the main drop-off points and compared high-retention vs low-retention users on behaviour and context (device, location, job category).

Analysis was done in SQL and Python; cohort and funnel views were built in the analytics stack and exported to Tableau for stakeholders.

Key findings

  • Five main drop-off points stood out: after first search (no click), after first job view (no apply), after first application (no return), and two in-app steps that I recommended product to instrument and revisit.
  • Users who applied at least once in the first 7 days had ~28% higher 30-day retention; early application was a strong leading indicator.
  • Mobile-only users had lower retention than desktop or multi-device; session length and depth were lower on mobile, suggesting UX or relevance issues on small screens.
  • Certain acquisition channels brought in users with meaningfully higher retention; reallocating spend toward those channels was recommended.

Recommendations

  • Improve first-session relevance (e.g. better default search, personalised results) to increase likelihood of an application in the first 7 days.
  • Add or refine email and in-app nudges at the identified drop-off points (e.g. after view-without-apply, after first application).
  • Run a mobile-focused UX review and prioritise fixes that increase session depth and application rate on mobile.
  • Shift acquisition budget toward channels with higher retained users, and run small tests before scaling.
